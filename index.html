The Real Picture

Most websites fail
disabled users — and
their owners don't know it.

96.3% of home pages have detectable WCAG failures. That's not a niche problem — it's a legal and business liability waiting to happen.

Where most sites are failing

  • Low contrast text — invisible to people with low vision and many older users
  • Missing alt text — images are completely silent for screen reader users
  • Keyboard traps — users who can't use a mouse get stuck and can't proceed
  • Inaccessible forms — unlabeled inputs leave users confused and unable to complete purchases
  • ADA/AODA lawsuits — web accessibility litigation hit a record 4,600+ cases in 2023

What we do about it

  • Full WCAG 2.2 audit — automated and manual, covering all 78 success criteria across your site
  • Hands-on remediation — our developers fix the code directly, not just give you a PDF report
  • Legal compliance documentation — VPATs, accessibility statements, and audit trails for legal protection
  • Team training — so your developers and designers never introduce the same issues again
  • Ongoing monitoring — we stay with you so regressions are caught before they become lawsuits

Proof of Work

Real audits.
Real results.

See the kinds of issues we find and the improvements we deliver. Every project ends with measurable, documented accessibility gains.

Critical
Product images missing alt text
847 product images have no alt attribute. Screen reader users cannot understand product offerings, directly impacting sales for 8% of your potential customers.
WCAG 1.1.1 Non-text Content (A)
Critical
Checkout form — unlabeled inputs
Payment form fields use placeholder text only as labels. When a user focuses an input, the placeholder disappears and the field purpose is lost — causing errors and abandoned checkouts.
WCAG 1.3.1 Info & Relationships (A), 3.3.2 Labels (A)
Warning
Color contrast failure on sale badges
White "#SALE" text on yellow (#FFD700) background achieves only 1.1:1 contrast ratio. Minimum required is 4.5:1. Effectively invisible to low-vision users.
WCAG 1.4.3 Contrast Minimum (AA)
Warning
Modal focus trap missing
Quick-view product modal does not trap keyboard focus. Tab key exits modal into background page, leaving keyboard users unable to interact with or close the dialog properly.
WCAG 2.1.2 No Keyboard Trap (A) — but inverse problem
Critical
Data table — no column headers
Analytics tables use <div> elements without <th> or scope attributes. Screen reader users hear raw numbers with no context about what each column represents.
WCAG 1.3.1 Info & Relationships (A)
Critical
Icon-only buttons with no accessible names
24 toolbar buttons use only SVG icons with no text or aria-label. Screen reader users hear "button button button" with no indication of what each button does.
WCAG 4.1.2 Name, Role, Value (A)
Warning
Toast notifications — not announced
Success/error toasts appear visually but are never announced to screen readers. User actions complete silently for AT users — they never know if their save succeeded.
WCAG 4.1.3 Status Messages (AA)
Info
Focus indicator missing on custom inputs
Custom-styled toggle switches and dropdowns suppress the browser's default focus ring with outline:none and provide no replacement. Keyboard users cannot tell which element has focus.
WCAG 2.4.7 Focus Visible (AA), 2.4.11 Focus Appearance (AAA)
Critical
Donation form — no error recovery
When form validation fails, errors are displayed visually but never communicated to screen readers. AT users submit the form, hear nothing, and don't know why the donation failed.
WCAG 3.3.1 Error Identification (A), 3.3.3 Error Suggestion (AA)
Warning
Video — no captions or transcript
4 impact story videos have no closed captions and no text transcripts. Deaf visitors and those with audio processing difficulties cannot access the content.
WCAG 1.2.2 Captions Pre-recorded (A)
Info
Language not set on translated pages
Spanish and French pages have lang="en" on the HTML element. Screen readers pronounce the content with wrong phonetics, making it incomprehensible to native speakers using AT.
WCAG 3.1.2 Language of Parts (AA)
